As a community, where are we at as librarians currently at? Throughout the 20th century, librarians have embedded new technologies within their profession. The introduction of Linked Data within the library community offers enormous potential for sharing information in a more structured manner on the Web. However, some of the conceptual and operational aspects of Linked Data seem exotic or even outright scandalous to a librarian. These ten modules will allow you to better understand the possibilities and challenges

Nile perch was artificially introduced in lakes across the African continent. This fish eats up all other fishes and completely wrecks the ecological balance. Are Linked Data colonizing and corrupting library metadata ?

Or can library metadata and Linked Data peacefully co-exist and leverage one another, as is proposed in the BIBFRAME model ? These ten modules will help you develop a more nuanced perspective on Linked Data by mastering some of the key methods and tools yourself!

Libraries and Linked Data

  • Share the same agenda
    • making information available in an open and structured manner
  • Have different world-views
    • Libraries create boutique metadata: carefully curated sets of high-quality cataloging records and controlled vocabularies
    • Linked Data are based on the open-world assumption and embrace the opportunities offered by big data
  • Integrating the different paradigms is challenging !

Goal of these modules

  • Librarians, be aware of IT fashion !
    • Technologies come and go at a fast pace, librarians should be capable to go beyond the hype and understand what fundamentally changes their profession and what not
  • Clearly identify the apples and the coconuts
    • Linked Data offer low-hanging fruits, bringing immediate gains at a relative small cost, but may also require heavy up-front investments which will not immediately pay off

Seeing beyond the hype

Gartner introduced the hype cycle which demonstrates how technologies get over-hyped when they are introduced. It sometimes takes 5-10 years before the read added-value of the technology becomes apparent and leads to operational advantages on the terrain.

Progress with Linked Data in Catalogs

Important work on both the cataloging rules and the format has taken place over the last few years :

  • Resource Description Access (RDA)
    • New cataloging rules replacing AACR2
  • Bibframe
    • New container format to replace MARC. Bibframe 2.0 was published in April 2016
Bibframe 2.0 was recently introduced and now features three levels of abstraction, referred to as core classes: Work, Instance and Item. However, it is still very much work in progress and the Library of Congress is working hard on providing more information and help on how to go forwards with the conversion from MARC to BIBFRAME. However, a lot of uncertainty and open-standing questions are outed internationally.
