Meet Rusty, the red panda! These animals prefer to stick very close to their home range, but the Smithsonian National Zoo posted this picture when Rusty went missing... Where are we at as librarians currently? We define ourselves through a substantial body of cataloging rules and formats, developed and fine-tuned over decades. The introduction of Linked Data within the library community offers enormous potential to publish metadata as first class citizens on the Web. However, there is still a lot of discomfort surrounding the usage of Linked Data in a library context...

The man on the picture is holding a Nile perch. This fish was artificially introduced in lake Victoria and ended up eating all other fishes and completely wrecked the ecological balance. Are Linked Data colonizing and corrupting the carefully curated library metadata ? Some of the conceptual and operational aspects of Linked Data seem exotic or even outright scandalous to a librarian.

Or can library metadata and Linked Data peacefully co-exist and leverage one another, as is proposed in the BIBFRAME model ? These ten modules will help you develop a more nuanced perspective on Linked Data by mastering some of the key methods and tools yourself!

Libraries and Linked Data

  • Share the same agenda
    • making information available in an open and structured manner
  • Have different world-views
    • Libraries have created boutique metadata: carefully curated sets of cataloging records and controlled vocabularies
    • Linked Data are based on the open-world assumption and embrace the opportunities offered by big data
  • Integrating the different paradigms is challenging !

Goal of these modules

  • Librarians, be aware of IT fashion !
    • Technologies come and go at a fast pace, librarians should be capable to go beyond the hype and understand what fundamentally changes their profession and what not
  • Clearly identify the apples and the bananas
    • Linked Data offer low-hanging fruits, bringing immediate gains at a relative small cost, but may also require heavy up-front investments in your library's information architecture

Seeing beyond the hype

Gartner introduced the hype cycle which demonstrates how technologies get over-hyped when they are introduced. It sometimes takes 5-10 years before the read added-value of the technology becomes apparent and leads to operational advantages on the terrain.

But wait... Why do we need a smarter Web ?

  • Looking for information on Picasso ?
    • Easy! Type Picasso into a search engine
  • Looking for paintings created by artists who were influenced by Picasso?

Both humans and machines need access to structured and semantically meaningfull data on the Web

A semantic Web is no fantasy, it is already a part of our daily Web experience. Analyze for example the search results based on the query "Drexel" from the right hand info box, where one can identify the basic structure of RDF: subject (Drexel), predicates (e.g. acceptance rate) and objects (e.g. 75%). This demonstrates how Linked Data are already very much a part of our day to day information consumption.

As much as search engines already manage to embed Linked Data, libraries are still strugling to come to terms with it. Despite the initial enthusiasm for the Semantic Web and Linked Data from a research perspective, it is often hard to find practicale examples which work. This video was published on Youtube in 2010, but it is still relevant. Despite promises from academics and consultants, we can not say that the Semantic Web has been a big success. One of the key purposes of these modules is to help you develop a more pragmatical view on what and what not to expect from Linked Data from the perspective as a librarian.

Even if the road towards the Semantic Web is difficult, it is no option to just stick with our former practices and tools ...

MARC has been an invaluable tool, but its card-based record-centric focus has also effectively locked our data in a tower and only those close aligned with the library profession have acces to the key.

F. Tim Knight, 2011

Progress with Linked Data in Catalogs

Important work on both the cataloging rules and the format has taken place over the last few years :

  • Resource Description Access (RDA)
    • New cataloging rules replacing AACR2
  • Bibframe
    • New container format to replace MARC and offering the possibility to embed Linked Data within a cataloging record
Bibframe 2.0 was recently introduced and now features three levels of abstraction, referred to as core classes: Work, Instance and Item. However, it is still very much a work in progress and the Library of Congress is working hard on providing more information and help on how to go forwards with the conversion from MARC to BIBFRAME.

Will Linked Data help you find a job ? Yes !

  • Metadata cleaning
    • The transition process underlines the importance of cleaning up inconsistencies in legacy metadata
  • Issuing identifiers
    • BIBFRAME requires the creation for example of IDs for Works, which libraries often do not have yet
  • Reconciliation and enriching
    • Creating automated links with knowledge bases stands high on the agenda

Structure of the training materials

  • Understanding the context
    • Three modules focus on the interaction between different data models, explain RDF and present the topic of data quality
  • Putting theory in practice
    • Three modules focus on concrete methods and tools to clean, reconcile and enrich metadata
  • Preparing for the future
    • The modules on REST and decentralisation & federation explain how the information architecture of your library should evolve
